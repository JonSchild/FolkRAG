6. It is never advisable to print rumors which are
phrased as epigrams, limericks, "gags", puns, etc.
The content may be denied, but the phrasing may
catch on.
7. Complicated arguments or cumbersome language are undesirable
in denying rumors. A rumor gains its impact
by being short, dramatic, and pointed. A
rambling or complex denial fails to nail the rumor
on the head and tends to give greater credibility and
circulation to the rumor.
8. In denying rumors, it is effective to use decisive 
words ("false", "absolutely false", "nonsense") at 
the beginning of the denial. Beginning the denial
with qualified phrases or an apologetic attitude gives
the rumor an aura of truth. It has been found dangerous
to start the denial with such phrases as, "It is
true that...", "We must admit that....", or "There
is some truth in the statement that....".
9. If a rumor is printed and is not denied flatly, then
the rumor has been given a wider public and increased
circulation; the controversial aspects of the rumor,
or the problem to which the rumor refers, may be kept
alive.

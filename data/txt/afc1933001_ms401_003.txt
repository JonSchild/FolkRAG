 Iron Head                       1934
Convict :#3610, aged sixty-three,


     I first saw Iron Head, late one (evening) when I was re-
cording Negro folk tunes in the hospital of the Central Farm
of the Texas Penitentiary System near Sugarland, Texas.  Cap-
tain Gotch had told me that Mexico (Ernest Williams), a Negro convict
and steward of the hospital, was the best singer on the farm.
Only two or three convalescents were in the hospital, so the recording
machine was set up in the [?] of their large [?] (near their beds where it was quiet).  While Mexico, (the hospital steward) was singing.  Among the curious
faces pressed against the window bars on the outside I saw
a low solidly built Negro listening intently. (to the singing)
This man beckoned me to (him).  "I'se Iron Head, a trusty," he
said.  "I knows lots of (those) 'jumped up' 'sinful' songs, more than [?] any 
of these niggers."
     That night and throughout the long day following, Iron-
Head proved that he did know the [regular?] songs of the black man.  He
and his "partner," Clear-Rock, turn and turn about, sang rhythm-
ic, surging songs of labor; songs of the jailbird; songs of the "bleed
hounds" tracking the fleeing Negro through river bottoms; of 
the bull whip ("Black Betty") and cowhide in the hands of an
angry "Cap'n"; songs of the loneliness and dismal monotony of life
in the penitentiary; songs of pathetic longing for his "doney"; songs of 
the bold black desperado with his trusty "forty-fo" in his
hand and with his enemy lying dead in the smoke pouring from
